Project Overview


Back to top
For our Senior Design project, we chose to examine the problem of resource distribution to counter the spread of epidemics. Our project aims to build off of previous research of our advisors to apply a model for cost-optimal distribution of resources throughout a networked population, given a fixed budget, to best constrain the spread of disease. Each tab of the website provides an interactive tool for public health officials to make better decisions on flu resource allocation, with data taken from Google Flu Trends.
The first tab, Flu Data by City, allows the user to graph the number of people infected out of 100,000 by city and year. A pop up allows users to also view total population and age distributions for each of these cities. The second tab, Heat Map, allows users to study how influential a city is in a selcted network across different times. Influence is defined by correlations observed in the data. Lastly, the Resource Allocation tab is the heart of the model, which allows the user choose a network of a US region--the Northeast, South, or West--and a total budget to be allocated across the cities.

Model Methodology


Back to top
The resource allocation model aims to determine how a specified budget can best be spent over a particular network of cities to contain the spread of the disease. Through a convex optimization problem, the Matlab engine connected to our website seeks to minimize the eigenvalue of a matrix representing the relationships between cities in the network. This is essentially equivalent to maximizing the recovery rates for the network, subject to budgetary constraints, seen in the optimization problem to the left.

Since the Google Flu Trends data represents an approximation of what the actual infections rates were in these years, the true proportions of the population infected are assumed to be the baseline. The budget input by the user represents any additional money spent across the network.

Model Assumptions


Back to top
1. Since it is unrealistic to assume that the recovery rate could ever be a perfect 100%, we have set the maximum possible recovery rate in a particular city to be 0.9.
2. The natural recovery rates of each city were calculate by running the model with a $0 budget to allocate, and minimizing the differences between the original and predicted data.
3. Based on the observed data trends, the flu seasons were selected to range from August of one year to July of the next. Each network in the resource allocation model has a specified maximum possible budget. This represents the amount of resources that if spent, would raise each city’s recovery rate to the maximum possible recovery rate. For the Northeast, South, and West, these assumed maximum budgets are $60M, $18M, and $30M, respectively based on the estimated cost to vaccinate 90% of the people in the cities of each network. The cost of vaccination was estimated to be $16.
4. “People Saved” is estimated by calculating the distance between the actual data before resource allocation and the projected data after allocation.
